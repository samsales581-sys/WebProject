Labor Rights

Republic Act 6727 · Labor Code of the Philippines
▶
In Simple Terms

Your employer must follow minimum wage laws and cannot make you work without proper pay.

  • You have the right to a minimum wage set by region
  • Overtime work (beyond 8 hrs) must be paid at 125% of your rate
  • Night shift differential (10pm–6am) adds 10% extra pay
  • You can join or form a labor union
  • Illegal dismissal can be contested before NLRC
  • You are entitled to 13th month pay every December
📋

Safe Working Conditions

Occupational Safety and Health Standards (DOLE DO 198-18)
▶
In Simple Terms

Your workplace must be safe. Employers cannot put your life at risk to save money.

  • Employers must provide free safety equipment (helmets, gloves, masks)
  • You can refuse to work if conditions are dangerous without fear of termination
  • DOLE can conduct surprise inspections on unsafe workplaces
  • Employers must have a safety officer and first-aid kit on site
  • Work-related injuries must be compensated through ECC/SSS
🔒

Data Privacy Rights

Republic Act 10173 · Data Privacy Act 2012
▶
In Simple Terms

Companies must ask your permission before collecting your personal information online.

  • You can ask any company what data they have about you
  • You can request deletion of your personal data
  • They must tell you within 72 hours if your data was leaked
  • You can file a complaint with the National Privacy Commission (NPC)
  • Companies cannot sell your data to third parties without consent
📱

Phone & Communication Privacy

RA 4200 · Anti-Wiretapping Law
▶
In Simple Terms

No one can record or listen to your private conversations without your permission.

  • Secret recording of calls or conversations is illegal
  • Employers cannot spy on your personal phone messages
  • Even government agents need a court order to wiretap
  • Evidence from illegal wiretapping cannot be used in court
🛒

Consumer Rights

Republic Act 7394 · Consumer Act
▶
In Simple Terms

You are safe from being cheated when buying products or services.

  • Products must be safe to use — defective ones must be replaced or refunded
  • Sellers must show correct and honest prices
  • False or misleading advertisements are illegal
  • You can file complaints with the DTI
  • Online sellers have the same obligations as physical stores
💳

Online Shopping & E-Commerce Rights

DTI MC 20-01 · E-Commerce Rules
▶
In Simple Terms

When you buy online, the seller still has legal responsibilities to you.

  • You have the right to accurate product photos and descriptions
  • Sellers must clearly state delivery timeframes and fees
  • You can cancel orders before shipment
  • You must receive a receipt or proof of purchase
  • Report scam sellers to DTI or your payment provider
🎓

Education Rights

Republic Act 9155 · Governance of Basic Education
▶
In Simple Terms

Every child in the Philippines has the right to free basic education.

  • Free public schooling from Kindergarten to Grade 12
  • Schools cannot collect illegal fees (DepEd Order 88-2010)
  • Students have the right to organize and be heard
  • No discrimination based on gender, religion, or disability
  • Bullying in school is prohibited under RA 10627
🏫

Free Higher Education Rights

Republic Act 10931 · Universal Access to Quality Tertiary Education Act
▶
In Simple Terms

Qualified students can attend State Universities and Colleges (SUCs) for free.

  • No tuition and other school fees in public universities (CHED-supervised)
  • Covers college and technical/vocational education
  • Private school students may qualify for TDP (Tertiary Education Subsidy)
  • Apply through your school's scholarship or financial aid office
🛡️

Cybercrime Protection

Republic Act 10175 · Cybercrime Prevention Act
▶
In Simple Terms

The law protects you from being harassed, scammed, or threatened online.

  • Online harassment and cyberbullying are criminal offenses
  • Identity theft online is punishable by up to 12 years in prison
  • You can report cybercrimes to the NBI Cybercrime Division or PNP-ACG
  • Unauthorized access (hacking) to your accounts is illegal
  • Online scams and phishing are prosecutable crimes
📸

Photo & Image Privacy Online

RA 9995 · Anti-Photo and Video Voyeurism Act
▶
In Simple Terms

No one can take, share, or spread your private photos or videos without your consent.

  • Sharing intimate images without consent is a crime ("revenge porn")
  • Penalty: 3–7 years imprisonment and up to ₱500,000 fine
  • Even if you originally shared the photo yourself, spreading it further is illegal
  • Report to NBI or PNP and request platform removal (Facebook, etc.)

Disability Rights

Republic Act 7277 · Magna Carta for Disabled Persons
▶
In Simple Terms

People with disabilities are protected from discrimination in all areas of life.

  • 20% discount on medicines, transport, restaurants, hotels
  • Equal job opportunities required — companies with 50+ staff must hire at least 1% PWDs
  • Public buildings must be PWD accessible (ramps, elevators)
  • Schools must accommodate and not discriminate against PWD students
  • Additional tax exemptions for PWDs and their caregivers
🏥

Senior Citizen & PWD Benefits

RA 9994 · Expanded Senior Citizens Act
▶
In Simple Terms

Senior citizens (60+) and PWDs enjoy special discounts and priority services by law.

  • 20% discount + VAT exemption on medicines, food, and utilities
  • Priority lanes in banks, hospitals, and government offices
  • Free flu and pneumonia vaccines in public health centers
  • Monthly social pension for indigent seniors (DSWD)
  • Philhealth automatically covers senior citizens
🏥

Universal Health Care Rights

Republic Act 11223 · Universal Health Care Act
▶
In Simple Terms

Every Filipino is automatically covered by PhilHealth for basic health services.

  • All Filipinos are automatically PhilHealth members — no need to register
  • Free outpatient consultations at public health centers
  • Free medicines for common illnesses in barangay health centers
  • No balance billing in government hospitals for indigent patients
  • Mental health services are covered under PhilHealth (RA 11036)
🧠

Mental Health Rights

Republic Act 11036 · Mental Health Act
▶
In Simple Terms

Your mental health matters and the law protects your right to care and dignity.

  • You have the right to mental health services in public hospitals
  • Employers must not discriminate against employees with mental health conditions
  • Schools must have mental health programs and counselors
  • You cannot be involuntarily hospitalized without due process
  • Confidentiality of mental health records is protected by law
🤱

Maternal Health & Breastfeeding Rights

RA 10028 · Expanded Breastfeeding Promotion Act
▶
In Simple Terms

Mothers have legal protections for pregnancy, childbirth, and breastfeeding at work.

  • 105-day maternity leave for working mothers (RA 11210)
  • Solo mothers get an extra 15 days of maternity leave
  • Employers must provide a lactation station (breastfeeding room)
  • Breastfeeding breaks during work hours are your legal right
  • Free prenatal care in public health centers
🏠

Housing Rights & Urban Poor

Republic Act 7279 · Urban Development and Housing Act
▶
In Simple Terms

You cannot be forcibly removed from your home without proper legal process.

  • Demolition without prior notice and relocation assistance is illegal
  • Eviction must follow legal court process — not just a landlord's order
  • You are entitled to 30 days advance notice before eviction
  • Informal settlers must be offered relocation sites before demolition
  • You can file complaints with HLURB (now DHSUD) for housing violations
🏢

Tenant Rights

Republic Act 9653 · Rent Control Act
▶
In Simple Terms

If you are renting a home, the landlord cannot just do whatever they want.

  • Landlords cannot raise rent more than 7% per year for units below ₱10,000/month
  • You cannot be evicted without a court order
  • Landlords must return your security deposit within 30 days of moving out
  • They must not cut off water and electricity to force you to leave
  • File complaints at HLURB or the barangay
👨‍👩‍👧

Children's Rights

Republic Act 7610 · Special Protection of Children
▶
In Simple Terms

Every child has the right to be safe, loved, educated, and protected from harm.

  • Child abuse, neglect, and exploitation are serious crimes
  • Child labor below 15 years old is illegal (with very limited exceptions)
  • Children in conflict with the law are treated separately under RA 9344 (JJWA)
  • Online child sexual abuse material (CSAM) is criminal under RA 9775
  • Report abuse to the DSWD, barangay, or police (WCPD unit)
🚨

Protection from Domestic Violence

Republic Act 9262 · Anti-VAWC Act
▶
In Simple Terms

Violence inside the home — physical, emotional, or financial — is a crime.

  • Covers violence against women and children by partners or family members
  • Includes physical, sexual, psychological, and economic abuse
  • You can apply for a Barangay Protection Order (BPO) immediately — for free
  • Violators can be arrested and jailed
  • Call the hotline: DSWD 8888 or Women's Crisis Center
⚧

Anti-Discrimination & Gender Equality

Republic Act 9710 · Magna Carta of Women
▶
In Simple Terms

Women have equal rights to men in all aspects of society — by law.

  • No discrimination in hiring, salary, promotion, or benefits based on gender
  • Women have equal property rights in marriage
  • Free legal assistance for women in gender-based violence cases
  • 30% of government positions should be held by women
  • Comprehensive health services for women are mandated by law
🚫

Anti-Sexual Harassment Rights

RA 7877 · Anti-Sexual Harassment Act · RA 11313 · Safe Spaces Act
▶
In Simple Terms

Sexual harassment anywhere — school, work, street, or online — is illegal.

  • Catcalling, wolf-whistling, and sexist remarks in public are punishable
  • Employers must create a sexual harassment committee
  • Online sexual harassment (sending unwanted explicit content) is a crime
  • Schools must have a designated officer handling harassment complaints
  • Penalties range from fines to imprisonment depending on severity
🌿

Right to a Clean Environment

RA 9275 · Clean Water Act · RA 8749 · Clean Air Act
▶
In Simple Terms

You have the right to clean air, safe water, and a healthy environment.

  • Industries cannot dump waste in rivers, lakes, or oceans
  • Burning of garbage in residential areas is prohibited
  • You can report pollution to DENR or local government
  • Communities near mining or industrial sites have the right to information about risks
  • Cutting protected trees without a permit is illegal (RA 9175)
♻️

Solid Waste & Plastic Pollution Rights

Republic Act 9003 · Ecological Solid Waste Management Act
▶
In Simple Terms

The government must manage garbage properly. You also have responsibilities.

  • LGUs must provide segregated waste collection (biodegradable, recyclable, residual)
  • Open dumpsites are prohibited — sanitary landfills are required
  • Littering in public is punishable by fines
  • Single-use plastic bags are regulated under many local ordinances
  • You can report illegal dumping to your barangay or LGU
🗳️

Voter Rights

Batas Pambansa 881 · Omnibus Election Code · RA 8189 · Voter's Registration Act
▶
In Simple Terms

Every Filipino citizen 18 and above has the right to vote and be counted.

  • You must register with COMELEC to vote (registration is free)
  • Your vote is secret — no one can force you to reveal who you voted for
  • Vote buying and selling are crimes — both giver and taker can be jailed
  • Voter intimidation and harassment are election offenses
  • Overseas Filipinos can vote through the OAV Law (RA 9189)
📢

Freedom of Speech & Expression

1987 Philippine Constitution · Article III, Section 4
▶
In Simple Terms

You have the right to speak your mind, but with limits to protect others.

  • The government cannot silence legitimate criticism or peaceful protest
  • Freedom of the press is constitutionally protected
  • You may freely practice your religion without government interference
  • Hate speech, libel, and incitement to violence are NOT protected
  • Peaceful assembly requires permit but cannot be unreasonably denied
